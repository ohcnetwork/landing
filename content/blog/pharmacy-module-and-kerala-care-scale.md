June 2025 expanded CARE HMIS through one of the most operationally important modules in any healthcare facility: pharmacy.

The pharmacy module connected clinical orders, inventory, stock movement, billing, and dispensing. In parallel, the Kerala Palliative Care Grid crossed major adoption milestones and received public recognition as a digital backbone for statewide palliative care.

## Building pharmacy as a complete workflow

Pharmacy work sits at the intersection of clinical care, supply chain, finance, and patient experience. CARE's pharmacy module was designed to support that full workflow rather than treat dispensing as an isolated action.

The module introduced:

- Medication orders through dynamic questionnaires.
- Prescriptions generated from patient encounters.
- Product knowledge for medications, consumables, nutritional supplements, strengths, units, and forms.
- Charge item definitions with pricing, taxes, and discount rules.
- Multiple pharmacy locations within a facility.
- Prescription queues filtered by encounter type and priority.
- Inventory items with stock levels, batch numbers, and expiry dates.
- Internal transfers, purchase orders, inward purchase entries, and stock condition tracking.
- Billing, payment recording, dispensing, and prescription printing.

This gave CARE a practical foundation for hospital pharmacy operations. It also linked pharmacy activity back to clinical encounters and patient accounts, which would become critical for later HMIS work.

## Making configuration faster

June also improved the questionnaire builder. Administrators could reorder choices, swap questions across groups, use conditional logic with multi-select dropdowns, and import or export questionnaire JSON.

These changes mattered because health deployments continuously adapt forms. A palliative care program, a hospital department, a lab workflow, and a screening campaign all require different data capture models. The faster a team can configure forms without engineering intervention, the more adaptable CARE becomes.

Other usability improvements included a built-in image cropper, a redesigned facility department view, rapid registration mode, and a better appointment slot picker.

## A public launch and operational scale

The Kerala Palliative Care Grid reached a major public milestone in June with a large launch event in Kochi. CARE was presented as the platform powering the grid, and a standard operating procedure was released to guide users.

By the end of June, adoption had reached remarkable scale:

- 1,365 government healthcare facilities onboarded.
- 1,105 NGO and community-based providers onboarded.
- More than 160,000 patients registered.
- More than 300,000 home visits recorded.

These numbers showed that CARE was supporting daily work across government and community care ecosystems. It had moved from pilot to operational infrastructure.

## Why this month mattered

June tied together two sides of CARE's future. On one side, the palliative care grid showed population-scale public health adoption. On the other, the pharmacy module showed that CARE could support the detailed operational workflows required inside hospitals.

The same core platform was learning from both environments. That became one of CARE's strongest product advantages: field-scale palliative care and department-scale hospital workflows could both strengthen the shared core.
