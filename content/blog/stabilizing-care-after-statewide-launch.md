April 2025 was the first full month after the statewide launch of the Kerala Palliative Care Grid. The focus shifted from launch readiness to operational stability.

That shift changed the nature of the work. CARE now had to support daily use across districts, respond to live feedback, and keep improving while real patients, nurses, doctors, and administrators depended on the system.

## CARE 3.0 becomes field-ready

The engineering team delivered a broad CARE 3.0 upgrade during April. The work improved the platform at multiple levels: user interface, clinical workflows, security, performance, and release reliability.

Key product improvements included:

- A simplified patient registration flow designed for high-volume settings.
- A home-based care module for nurses to record vitals, symptoms, diagnoses, medicines, and follow-up notes during home visits.
- A ShadCN UI refresh that improved spacing, consistency, accessibility, and mobile responsiveness.
- Role-based access control with instance-specific roles.
- Device management for equipment such as monitors, ventilators, and wearables.
- Location and bed management down to buildings, floors, rooms, and beds.
- Printable treatment and discharge summaries.
- Consent management within patient records.
- Fetching past medicines, symptoms, and diagnoses from previous encounters.
- Questionnaire import and export with value-set bindings.

The result was a platform that felt less like a set of pilot features and more like a production system prepared for diverse healthcare workflows.

## Security and access control mature

April also introduced two-factor authentication and stronger role-based controls. This was essential because CARE was now handling sensitive personal health data across a live statewide deployment.

Security in healthcare is not only about keeping attackers out. It is also about making sure the right person sees the right information at the right time. Nurses, doctors, administrators, district teams, and state teams need different levels of access. CARE's role system began giving deployments that control without requiring every change to be hardcoded by engineers.

## Operational scale becomes visible

The Kerala Palliative Care Grid recorded around 80,000 new patient registrations and more than 84,000 home-care visits during April alone. That usage demonstrated that the grid had moved beyond symbolic launch activity.

Daily adoption by nurses and medical officers created a constant stream of real-world feedback. The team maintained operational support, ran reviews with implementation teams, and continued improving the product based on district-level usage.

This was also the month when onboarding of NGOs and community-based organizations began moving into the broader vision. CARE was being prepared not just for public facilities, but for a wider care network that could include community organizations and volunteers.

## Lessons from scale

April made the implementation challenges clear. Field staff had varied levels of digital literacy. Some regions had connectivity constraints. Older smartphones affected performance. Training and support across departments required careful coordination.

But these constraints were useful. They forced CARE to become more practical. Mobile-first design, faster registration, inline validation, documentation videos, and responsive support all became part of the platform's quality bar.

By the end of April, CARE had crossed an important threshold. It had survived the first intense month of statewide use and emerged stronger, more secure, and more closely shaped by frontline healthcare workers.
