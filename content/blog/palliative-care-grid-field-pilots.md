January 2025 was the month CARE entered the field for the Kerala Palliative Care Grid. After months of preparation, the platform was tested in real palliative care workflows with nurses, doctors, staff, patients, and home-care teams.

The first pilots were deliberately hands-on. The goal was not to prove that a feature list existed. It was to learn whether community health teams could use CARE in the messy reality of home visits, mobile devices, network constraints, and paper-heavy routines.

## Ayyampuzha as the first field test

The pilot at Ayyampuzha Grama Panchayath was completed on 31 January 2025. The team worked closely with local healthcare providers and conducted in-person training for the community nurse, doctor, and support staff.

That training was followed by actual home visits. The implementation team accompanied the home-care team, observed how care was delivered, and supported documentation in CARE as the visits happened.

The feedback was immediate and practical. Nurses saw value in having patient data available digitally, especially when compared with maintaining paper-based records across repeated home visits. At the same time, the field context exposed friction points in mobile usability, data entry, and workflow sequence.

Those learnings translated into product improvements quickly. The mobile experience was refined, common actions were made easier, and the team began treating frontline usability as a core platform requirement rather than a later polish task.

## Expanding to Calicut Corporation

The second pilot began in Calicut Corporation on 24 January 2025. This introduced CARE into a larger urban local body context, with primary palliative care nurses trained across the corporation.

The Calicut work helped validate that CARE could support more than a single small pilot site. Nurses were trained, field assistance was provided, and the system began supporting routine documentation for palliative care operations.

By the end of the month, early adoption metrics were visible:

- 177 patients registered in the grid.
- 189 home visits recorded through CARE.

For a new digital workflow in a community care setting, those numbers were encouraging. More importantly, they created a live feedback loop between patient care, nurse experience, and software improvement.

## Training as a scaling strategy

The team also began training students involved in palliative care initiatives so they could support nurses in the field. This was an important operational decision. Large-scale health software adoption is not only an engineering challenge. It depends on repeated training, local confidence, and trusted support near the point of care.

Demo videos were created for both desktop and mobile workflows. This helped users revisit steps after training and reduced dependency on one-to-one support for every question.

## HMIS discovery begins in parallel

While the palliative care pilots moved forward, CARE HMIS discovery continued. A detailed visit to Sudheendra Mission Hospital in Kochi helped the team study hospital workflows and gather requirements across departments such as billing, laboratory services, and pharmacy.

This dual-track work was significant. The palliative care grid was testing CARE in distributed community workflows. The HMIS discovery was testing whether CARE could support the structured, high-volume operations of a hospital.

Both streams reinforced the same lesson: CARE had to be configurable, mobile-friendly, standards-aligned, and grounded in real workflows. January turned that lesson from a planning principle into field evidence.
