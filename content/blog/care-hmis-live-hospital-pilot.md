September 2025 was a milestone month for CARE HMIS. The pilot at Sree Sudheendra Medical Mission Hospital in Kochi was launched on 10 September, bringing CARE into a live multi-specialty hospital environment.

The hospital context was substantial: around 200 beds, 26 clinical departments, 6,000 to 7,000 outpatient consultations per month, and roughly 500 inpatient consultations per month. This was not a demo environment. It was a real hospital with active patient flow, departmental constraints, legacy habits, and high expectations.

## Multi-department onboarding

The implementation team worked on-site across major hospital departments to operationalize CARE and train staff.

Key onboarding areas included:

- Hospital management and administrative teams for oversight and reporting.
- Reception staff for registration, appointment booking, queue management, and billing.
- ENT department workflows with doctors and nursing staff.
- Casualty workflows for emergency registration, triage, and encounter management.
- Pharmacy workflows for dispensing, billing, inventory, and product setup.
- Laboratory workflows for specimen definitions, observation definitions, test configuration, and reporting.

Each department needed training, configuration, and live handholding. CARE had to support existing patient flow while gradually replacing fragmented digital and paper processes.

## Scheduling, lab, pharmacy, and billing mature together

September engineering work strengthened many modules at once. The scheduling module gained a unified framework for doctors, nurses, diagnostic rooms, and service points. Appointment screens were restructured, queues became more reliable, and calendar performance improved for large appointment volumes.

Pharmacy and inventory gained better stock returns, expiry-based lot selection, improved dispensing interfaces, and smoother procurement flows. Laboratory workflows gained barcode and QR support for specimen tracking, diagnostic report templates, and observation interpretations based on reference ranges.

Billing and accounts gained stronger validation, charge item management, invoice reconciliation, discount logic, and mobile-responsive interfaces.

This mattered because hospital workflows rarely stay inside one module. A patient registration affects the queue. A consultation creates lab and pharmacy requests. A lab test affects billing and reporting. CARE had to connect these flows coherently.

## Kerala CARE continues to scale

Alongside the HMIS pilot, the Kerala Palliative Care Grid continued expanding. By the end of September, CARE had recorded 218,000 patient registrations and 649,000 home visits.

Dashboard work also began for facility-level visibility, helping administrators review patient load, visits, and service utilization. Demo and training sessions were conducted to prepare for upcoming platform updates across the palliative care network.

## A proof point for CARE HMIS

The Sudheendra pilot validated CARE in a demanding live setting. Staff adoption depended on simplicity, not feature volume. Departments needed small-batch training because hospital operations could not pause for software rollout. Legacy systems continued in parallel during the transition, which made consistency and confidence essential.

September proved that CARE could move from codebase to hospital floor. It also created the feedback loop that would shape the next phase: every live workflow, exception, and staff question became input for improving CARE HMIS.
