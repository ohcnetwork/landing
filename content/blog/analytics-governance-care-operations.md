March 2026 showed CARE moving beyond implementation into measurable operational maturity. Sree Sudheendra Medical Mission Hospital had stabilized after two months of HMIS usage, while Kerala CARE continued to advance its governance and review systems.

The product work reflected this shift. Instead of focusing only on launch-critical fixes, CARE expanded analytics, interoperability, workflow efficiency, and governance controls.

## Independent hospital operations

By March, the HMIS deployment at Sree Sudheendra was operating without continuous hands-on support. Departments could manage routine workflows independently, with the team stepping in for targeted training, edge cases, and iterative improvements.

Refresher sessions continued, but the focus had changed. Instead of basic onboarding, training reinforced newer features and clarified workflow details. Hospital management collaborated with the team on reports and dashboards that would support review cycles and continuous improvement.

The implementation team also began creating Sudheendra-specific documentation and process-flow videos. This turned field knowledge into reusable training material, reducing long-term dependency on live support.

## Scan-based check-in and faster queues

March introduced QR-based patient scanning and appointment status validation directly within the encounter header. This made patient check-in faster and reduced manual search effort at busy front desks.

Queue visibility also improved through clearer token status indicators, mobile-friendly queue layouts, and contextual tooltips for common queue actions.

In high-volume healthcare settings, speed is not just convenience. A few saved clicks at reception, pharmacy, or nursing stations can reduce waiting time, lower staff stress, and make digital workflows feel more natural than paper.

## Data and interoperability

LOINC mapping began for lab orders to standardize activity definitions and improve interoperability. Diagnostic reference ranges became easier to interpret, and service request cards gained richer clinical details such as body site and patient instructions.

These updates strengthened CARE's long-term data layer. A mature health platform must do more than display data. It should capture structured, comparable, standards-aligned information that can support analytics, reporting, clinical review, and future integrations.

## Dashboards for hospitals and palliative care

March brought major analytics work for both HMIS and the palliative grid.

For hospital reporting, CARE built structured data pipelines and dashboards for pharmacy, finance, sales, purchases, doctor-wise charge item analysis, daily operations, and stock visibility. These dashboards helped hospital leadership review operational performance and financial activity with more precision.

For palliative care, dashboards were expanded to track first virtual and home health encounters, active patient definitions, appointment distribution, completed encounters, public dashboard metrics, volunteer linkage, mobility status, care coverage, procedures, diagnoses, data quality, and referrals.

## Governance structures for Kerala CARE

The Kerala Palliative Care Grid also advanced its role and responsibility framework. A state-level review on 9 March gathered feedback from districts after a year of usage. On 24 March, priorities for the next phase were discussed with NHM leadership.

The responsibility feature was completed to manage roles and hierarchy from state to ward level. This created a clearer structure for access, accountability, and program management.

March positioned CARE as a platform entering its next phase: less about proving it can launch, and more about proving it can be governed, measured, and improved at scale.
